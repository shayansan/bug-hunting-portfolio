# My Learning Motivation

From a young age, I have always had a strong curiosity about computers, especially the security side of them. This interest did not start in a structured or academic way. It started from simple real experiences.

For example, when I used to install games, the antivirus would sometimes flag and delete certain files. Instead of just being annoyed, I became curious about what was actually happening behind the scenes. Why were those files being detected? What was inside them that I couldn’t see?

Later, when I used cheat codes in games, my curiosity became even deeper. I was not only interested in the outcome of the cheat itself. I was more interested in understanding what was happening internally in the system when that code was executed.
Questions like:
What changes are happening in the background?
Is something being modified silently?
Is it possible to perform an action without visible manipulation?

This curiosity was not something temporary or trend-based. It was a natural and long-term interest that grew with me over time.

Because of this, my path toward cybersecurity is not based on hype or shortcuts.
It is based on a genuine curiosity about how systems behave beneath the surface and how hidden processes actually work.

My goal is not just to “study cybersecurity” as a subject, but to turn this long-standing curiosity into a professional and deeply structured career path.

# My Philosophy About GitHub and Documentation

For me, GitHub is not a place to collect notes or show activity.
It is a representation of my real knowledge and actual understanding.

If a repository is filled just to look active, attract attention, or impress someone for a job, but the understanding behind it is shallow, it will eventually become a weakness. Especially in cybersecurity, lack of depth in fundamentals creates serious problems in later stages.

I believe that documentation should only reflect what I truly understand, not what I have just read or quickly summarized.

That is why I deliberately avoid rushing content into my GitHub.
I prefer to spend more time studying, practicing, and deeply understanding a concept before documenting it.

My approach is slow but intentional.
Instead of producing a large amount of low-quality or surface-level material, I focus on building a strong and reliable foundation.

I see GitHub as a long-term technical portfolio, not a note-dumping platform.
Every file I add should represent real comprehension, not temporary exposure to a topic.

From the beginning of this journey, I am willing to invest more time to ensure depth, clarity, and long-term stability in my knowledge, rather than chasing speed or external validation.

# How I Study and Understand Technical Topics

When I start learning a new technical topic, the first thing I try to do is understand the big picture.
I want to know what the topic is about, what it actually does, and what its main sub-parts are. Before going deep into details, I try to build a mental map of the subject.

After I have that general understanding, I identify the parts that are relevant to my field of interest. Since my focus is cybersecurity and bug bounty, I try to filter the material and focus more on concepts that will eventually support that direction.

At that point I begin studying the topic step by step. Most of the time I prefer learning from books because in many technical areas, especially cybersecurity, books tend to explain concepts more deeply and systematically. My learning process usually becomes a combination of studying and practicing whenever practice is possible.

For topics that can be tested directly, I try to experiment with them. For topics that are more conceptual, like networking, I try to build a clear mental model and visualize how things work internally.

---

# My Approach to Deep Understanding

Over time I realized that some fields, especially networking, contain a lot of details and technical complexity. If I focus too much on details from the beginning, it becomes harder to understand the real structure of the system.

Because of that, I usually try to extract the core concepts first. My priority is to understand the main idea and the underlying structure of a system before diving into deeper details. Once the foundation becomes clear, I gradually explore the details only as much as they are actually needed.

While studying, I also try to read in a more analytical way rather than just moving forward in the text. I often pause and think about what I just read and how the different parts connect to each other.

---

# How I Verify My Understanding

After studying a concept, I usually go through the entire process again in my mind. I try to mentally reconstruct how the system or mechanism works and check if every step makes sense to me.

If something is still unclear, I don't move forward immediately. Instead, I try to investigate the topic further until it becomes clear. This might include asking new questions, revisiting the material, or searching for additional explanations.

If a concept can be demonstrated through practical examples, I actively try to find those examples. Real scenarios and demonstrations often help me understand a topic much more clearly.

If practical examples are not available, I look for other explanations using different resources such as videos, alternative documentation, or sometimes AI-based explanations that can help clarify the concept from another perspective.

---

# My Rule About Moving Forward

One personal rule I follow is that I do not move to the next section of a topic until I feel that the current one makes sense to me.

This doesn't necessarily mean understanding every tiny detail, but I need to at least understand the structure and the logic behind the concept before moving forward.

---

# My Personal Levels of Understanding

I usually evaluate my understanding using three levels.

The highest level is when I can clearly explain the concept to someone else. If I can teach or explain an idea in simple terms, it usually means I truly understand it.

If that is not possible yet, the next level is being able to apply the concept practically and interact with it in a real environment.

If neither of those is possible, I still try to at least reach the point where I understand the overall structure and how the system works conceptually.

These levels help me measure whether I actually understand something or whether I have only been exposed to it superficially.

---
# My Documentation Philosophy

When it comes to documentation, I do not write things immediately after reading them.

I prefer to wait until I feel that I have developed a deeper understanding of the concept. Only then do I start documenting it.

My goal with documentation is not simply to summarize information. Instead, I want to create a record of my learning process and build a personal technical reference.

In the long term, I also want this repository to serve as something I can refer others to. For example, if I apply for internships or collaborate with others, I want to be able to show a structured path of what I have learned and how I approached learning it.
