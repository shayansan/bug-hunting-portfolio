# My Learning Motivation

From a young age, I have always had a strong curiosity about computers, especially the security side of them. This interest did not start in a structured or academic way. It started from simple real experiences.

For example, when I used to install games, the antivirus would sometimes flag and delete certain files. Instead of just being annoyed, I became curious about what was actually happening behind the scenes. Why were those files being detected? What was inside them that I couldn’t see?

Later, when I used cheat codes in games, my curiosity became even deeper. I was not only interested in the outcome of the cheat itself. I was more interested in understanding what was happening internally in the system when that code was executed.
Questions like:
What changes are happening in the background?
Is something being modified silently?
Is it possible to perform an action without visible manipulation?

This curiosity was not something temporary or trend-based. It was a natural and long-term interest that grew with me over time.

Because of this, my path toward cybersecurity is not based on hype or shortcuts.
It is based on a genuine curiosity about how systems behave beneath the surface and how hidden processes actually work.

My goal is not just to “study cybersecurity” as a subject, but to turn this long-standing curiosity into a professional and deeply structured career path.

# My Philosophy About GitHub and Documentation

For me, GitHub is not a place to collect notes or show activity.
It is a representation of my real knowledge and actual understanding.

If a repository is filled just to look active, attract attention, or impress someone for a job, but the understanding behind it is shallow, it will eventually become a weakness. Especially in cybersecurity, lack of depth in fundamentals creates serious problems in later stages.

I believe that documentation should only reflect what I truly understand, not what I have just read or quickly summarized.

That is why I deliberately avoid rushing content into my GitHub.
I prefer to spend more time studying, practicing, and deeply understanding a concept before documenting it.

My approach is slow but intentional.
Instead of producing a large amount of low-quality or surface-level material, I focus on building a strong and reliable foundation.

I see GitHub as a long-term technical portfolio, not a note-dumping platform.
Every file I add should represent real comprehension, not temporary exposure to a topic.

From the beginning of this journey, I am willing to invest more time to ensure depth, clarity, and long-term stability in my knowledge, rather than chasing speed or external validation.
